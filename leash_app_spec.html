01

The concept

One job, done convincingly enough that people stop scrolling.

You give an AI agent a task and a budget. To do the task it needs paid resources — search, data feeds, model calls, compute. Instead of pre-paying subscriptions, the agent pays per use via x402 micropayments on XRPL from a wallet you fund with an allowance.

Your phone is the control tower: set the policy, watch every cent live, approve anything over a threshold, and hit a kill switch when you want. The demo task is a research agent — ask a question, it spends small amounts to pull paid results, returns a report, and shows exactly what it spent with real on-chain tx hashes you can open in a block explorer. Provable, autonomous, controlled — that's the moment that lands.

03

The payment loop

The technical heart, and a genuine sequence — build a thin slice of this end to end (one agent, one paid endpoint, one payment on the explorer) before any polish.

1
Agent needs a resource — mid-task, it decides it needs paid data.
2
Endpoint returns 402 — with payment details.
3
Policy engine intercepts — under caps? budget left? service allowed? not halted? over approval threshold?
4
Ask the human if required — emits approval_required → phone shows Approve / Deny → waits.
5
Sign & broadcast on XRPL — xrpl.js Payment; capture tx hash; present x402 proof.
6
Resource validates proof — returns the data.
7
Log + push live — amount, service, hash, ledger index, what it bought → mobile.
8
Continue — until task done or budget/policy stops it.

05

The policy engine

Your actual differentiator. Everyone builds "an agent that can pay." Almost nobody builds "an agent that pays under control" — and that's what you talk about when you sell the skill. Every payment passes these gates, in order:

GATE 1
Not halted
GATE 2
Service allowed
GATE 3
Per-tx cap
GATE 4
Daily cap
GATE 5
Total budget
GATE 6
Below approval threshold — else ask the human

Anything that fails is refused before a signature is ever produced. This is the part that turns a scary autonomous spender into something a person would actually trust.
